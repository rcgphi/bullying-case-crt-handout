Pepper Tree Elementary Racist Incident (2023)

Interactive handout • Analyzing a P–12 racial incident through CRT lenses

Case Overview

In February 2023, several racist bullying incidents at Pepper Tree Elementary School (Upland, California) shocked the community. Black students were targeted with hand-drawn pictures containing racial slurs and demeaning imagery — including drawings labeled “You’re my favorite monkey” and “cotton picker.” One parent reported that students even threatened to depict a Black child hanging from a tree.

The district condemned the acts as “racially insensitive and unacceptable,” citing a zero-tolerance policy for hate speech while pledging investigations and discipline. Yet the incident raised deeper concerns about the school’s climate, the persistence of racism among children, and whether anti-bias measures are working.

The case invites reflection on how schools respond to racism not as isolated incidents but as indicators of systemic and cultural forces influencing even young children.

1

Systemic Racism

What does this incident reveal about systemic or cultural racism influencing children? How might societal stereotypes and curriculum omissions (e.g., lack of Black history or anti-bias education) contribute to such behavior?

2

Counter‑Storytelling

As a school leader, how might you use counter‑storytelling to center the voices of Black students and families? What forums or curricular changes could help tell the “other side” of the story—teaching the history and harm of these slurs and celebrating Black excellence?

3

Interest Convergence & Intersectionality

Analyze the district’s response through an interest convergence lens: was the reaction motivated by true equity goals or by protecting the school’s reputation? How can justice for the targeted student align with broader community interests in safety and belonging? Consider intersectionality: the student targeted is a young Black girl—how should the response address her unique position across race, age, and gender?

Exit Reflection

What is one concrete action schools can take to prevent similar incidents and build a more inclusive climate for all children?
